# Initial Notes and Decisions

**Date:** 2026.05.02
**Status:** Captures strategic and architectural decisions made during initial planning, with reasoning and open questions surfaced.

This doc holds the load-bearing reasoning for the project. The MVP sketch (separate doc) inherits these decisions and should not re-litigate them.

---

## 1. Project framing

**Goal:** Build a strong AI agent for 8-player FFA on generals.io.

**Why this is interesting:** No published strong FFA bot exists. Strakam & Schmid's 2025 1v1 agent reached top 0.003% of the human ladder, but FFA is unaddressed. User is a high-ranked FFA player and well-positioned to evaluate.

**Core approach:** Behavioral cloning on expert FFA replays, then (eventually) self-play PPO fine-tuning. Modeled loosely on the Straka & Schmid 2025 1v1 recipe but with FFA-specific adaptations.

**Why FFA is not "1v1 with extra context":** Strategic decision-making differs structurally — attack timing has to account for third-party retaliation, target choice trades off position vs. army-strength vs. likely retaliation patterns, expansion patterns are shaped by which neighbor to spook least. Strong 1v1 players bring transferable fundamentals but have new strategic skills to learn. This shapes several downstream decisions (skip 1v1 BC, FFA-native observation design).

## 2. Algorithmic approach

### Decision: Skip AlphaZero-style methods

Generals lacks the three properties that make MCTS principled: perfect information, alternating turns, deterministic transitions. AlphaZero-style tabula-rasa learning works because MCTS itself is the bootstrap — it produces provably-improved policy targets via real lookahead into real future states. That guarantee weakens significantly under fog of war and simultaneous moves.

BC + PPO is search-free, model-free, handles partial observability without special machinery. Schmid (co-author of Player of Games and Student of Games) reached for PPO when *he* built a generals env. Strong signal we're on the right path.

### Decision: BC + self-play PPO recipe, modeled on Straka & Schmid 2025

Two stages:
1. **Behavioral cloning** on expert human replays establishes a competent starting policy. Substitute for the bootstrap that MCTS provides in AlphaZero — without it, PPO from scratch on sparse-reward generals is intractable.
2. **Self-play PPO** fine-tunes against historical opponent pool. Pushes policy beyond human imitation.

### Decision: Skip 1v1 BC pretraining; train BC directly on FFA replays

The argument for 1v1 BC was sample efficiency on fundamentals. The arguments against:

- Fundamentals are present in FFA replays too (every FFA game has thousands of frames of basic army management, expansion, fog reading).
- 1v1-pretrained policy is *miscalibrated* for FFA — it learns threat-evaluation circuits that assume one opponent. Fine-tuning has to unlearn that, possibly slower than learning correctly the first time. Negative transfer is real.
- Endgame argument cuts both ways but is weaker than it sounds. FFA collapses to 1v1 at the end, and 1v1 skill matters there. But endgame positions exist in the FFA corpus too — sampling issue, not a data-source issue. Upsample endgame frames from FFA replays to address this directly.

**Caveat — could verify empirically:** Train small BC models on 1v1-only and FFA-only, compare next-action prediction accuracy on FFA frames. If 1v1 transfers well even early-game, this decision should be revisited. Not done yet, low priority unless BC underperforms.

### Decision: Borrow strakam 1v1 agent only as endgame sparring during eventual self-play

When self-play reaches the last-two-players state, occasionally play out 1v1 endgames against the strakam agent (or a clone). Their published agent is top 0.003% at 1v1 — provides a strong, stable, diverse opponent for a sub-skill that's hard to elicit from FFA self-play alone.

Concretely: sample some fraction of self-play games (5-10%) starting from curated late-game 1v1 positions, with strakam as opponent. Cheap addition, addresses the "FFA endgame is 1v1" concern without paying negative-transfer costs.

## 3. MVP scope and deferral decisions

### Decision: Phase 1 MVP is BC-only; engine refactor and self-play deferred to Phase 2+

**Phase definitions used throughout this doc:**
- **Phase 1 (MVP):** Replay parser, BC training, FFA-aware `generalsio_state.py`, live-ladder deployment.
- **Phase 2 (if BC is weak):** Engine refactor (`game.py` → FFA), self-play PPO infrastructure, RL fine-tuning.
- **Phase 3 (if Phase 2 succeeds):** Tuning, league play if coordination becomes an issue, polish.

**Critical insight:** BC doesn't need an FFA-aware engine. It's pure supervised learning on `(observation, action)` tuples from parsed replays. As long as the replay parser outputs tensors in the target FFA observation format, BC trains end-to-end without touching `game.py`.

The env is needed for:
- Self-play training (deferred to Phase 2)
- Simulated evaluation (can be replaced with live-ladder evaluation)

Live-ladder evaluation needs `generalsio_state.py` made FFA-aware (~days of work — replace 1v1 hardcoding on line 10, generalize observation construction). This is bounded work; the full engine refactor (`game.py` rewrite, ~weeks) is not on the critical path.

**Phase 1 sequence:**

1. Replay parser → FFA observation tensors (user's side project, in progress)
2. BC training loop on parser output
3. FFA-aware `generalsio_state.py`
4. Deploy to live ladder, measure
5. Decision point: BC strong enough? Or escalate to Phase 2?

**Highest-risk dependency in Phase 1: parser correctness.** If the parser produces subtly wrong observations, BC silently learns the wrong things, and we don't find out until live deployment looks bad. End-to-end sanity checks (e.g., reconstruct a known game state from parser output, compare to ground truth) are worth investing in early — much cheaper than debugging a mistrained network downstream.

### Decision: Phase 2 only if BC results warrant it

Probability distribution on BC-alone outcomes (Claude's rough estimates):
- 30% — decent FFA bot, possibly the strongest published, plays competently but not at top-human level
- 40% — weak-mid, validates pipeline, RL needed
- 20% — plateaus disappointingly, debugging adventure
- 10% — genuinely strong, top-quartile human play, RL only marginal

The phase-2 decision will be informed by real deployment data, not hypothetical training curves. Strong outcome → ship and tune. Weak outcome → start engine refactor + self-play.

## 4. Data and replays

### Decision: BC corpus is top-player FFA replays from the AWS bucket

User has ~35k collected, easily scaled to ~100k with another scraping pass. Quality > quantity at this scale; expect 30%+ loss to filtering, so the post-filter usable corpus is meaningfully smaller than raw count.

### Decision: Filter aggressively but simply for MVP

Three filters:
1. **Integrity cuts** (AFK, disconnect, incomplete) — required, in progress
2. **Early elimination of target** (before turn ~50-75) — drops noisiest data; param tunable
3. **Bottom-half placement of target** — strong players' off-games contain mistakes BC will faithfully imitate

Skip for MVP: map-feature filtering, time/recency filters, hand-tuned game-quality scoring, opponent-strength filtering. Premature without ground truth.

### Decision: Capture rich per-trajectory metadata at parse time, filter at training time

Parse output is `(obs, action, metadata)` where metadata includes player_id, player_rating, placement, game_length, elim_turn, game_id, turn-within-game.

Why: lets you toggle filters via config without re-parsing; enables placement-weighted loss, conditional policies, eval splits, and debugging-by-replay-lookup later. Re-parsing 100k replays is slow; capturing metadata once is cheap. Single highest-leverage data infrastructure decision.

### Open question: Top-k cloning policy

Three options:
- Winner-only (cleanest signal, smallest dataset, survivorship bias)
- Top-k (probably top 3) — bigger dataset, captures placement skill, mixes "win" and "survive 4th"
- Player-conditioned all-8 (richest, most flexible, more architectural complexity)

Lean: start with top-3, consider player-conditioned later if useful for style-conditioned league diversity.

### Open question: Endgame upsampling

A 1800-turn game has ~1800 early-game positions and ~50 endgame positions. Endgame skill matters disproportionately (1v1 collapse). Vanilla uniform sampling under-weights it. Likely fix: turn-based weighting in the dataloader. Exact form TBD.

### Assumption to validate: ~50-100k post-filter top-player replays is sufficient for BC

Starting from ~35k raw, scaling to ~100k+ if needed. After filtering (~30%+ loss), realistic post-filter corpus is in the 25-70k range from current data. Per top-player game produces 1000+ player-trajectory steps, so even the lower end gives plenty of supervised samples for a 5-15M parameter network. Marginal returns past this point are likely modest; quality and diversity matter more than raw count.

## 5. Architecture (provisional, deferred to dedicated session)

### Decision: One network, shared trunk, multiple heads

Standard pattern (AlphaZero, AlphaStar, modern actor-critic). Trunk does spatial feature extraction; heads read off for specific predictions:
- **Policy head** — action distribution (action-masked)
- **Value head** — scalar value / placement
- **Auxiliary heads** (optional) — placement prediction, attack-target prediction, who-eliminates-whom

BC trains all heads via supervised losses. PPO continues training all heads via actor-critic. Auxiliary heads shape trunk representation, don't change inference.

### Decision: Player-relative observations

Network does not see "player 3 owns this, player 7 owns that." It sees "I own this, my biggest threat owns that, second-biggest threat owns this other one." Sort opponents by some scalar (army count, land count, or learned threat score) before feeding.

Makes the network position-invariant to player slot. Lets it generalize across all 8 perspectives in a game. Required for sensible training; not optional.

### Decision: Network size in the 5-15M parameter range

Game RL networks stay small for good reasons: small board, structured observations (not raw pixels), inference speed matters during self-play, BC is data-limited.

Anchors: AlphaZero Go ~20M, AlphaStar ~140M (much bigger because of unit handling), KataGo-like ResNets 5-30M. Strakam network is in the single-digit-millions range.

Starting point: ResNet-style trunk with 8-12 residual blocks, channel width 128-256. Two small heads. Scale up only if BC validation loss says we're underfitting (rare).

### Open questions (deferred to architecture session)

- Per-cell channel design — extending strakam's 14 channels for FFA. Per-opponent ownership masks? Last-seen army counts in fog? Time-since-seen?
- Opponent representation — how many slots to expose (top 3? all 7?), sorted by what
- Global features — how to expose leaderboard scalars (broadcast as constant channels? separate vector head input?)
- Memory mechanism — LSTM, transformer over a window, stacked frames; coupled to fog-of-war handling
- Exact backbone — ResNet, ViT-style, U-Net

**Coupling to data pipeline:** The parser's output schema and the network's input schema are the same decision. The architecture session has a soft deadline embedded in it — decisions need to be made before the parser output schema is locked in, or we pay re-parsing costs on the existing replay corpus.

## 7. FFA-specific design considerations

### Coordination is *not* a primary concern

User input: explicit alliances are rare in FFA. Most coordination is implicit and emerges only in last-3 situations. Players occasionally negotiate truces in chat but with no enforcement (trust-based, infrequent).

Implication: simpler historical-pool self-play is sufficient. League play (AlphaStar-style with specialized exploiters) is overkill — its purpose is eliciting diverse strategic types and emergent coordination, neither of which is the bottleneck here.

The behaviors that actually matter (retaliation, boundary testing, threat-aware aggression, leaderboard reading) are all things BC handles well from the corpus. Self-play sharpens them.

**To revisit:** if BC produces an agent that gets ganged up on by humans who recognize bot behavior, coordination handling may need to come back into scope.

### Research direction: threat-conditioned BC heads (speculative)

Strategic mode shifts genuinely between "I'm leading," "I'm middle of pack," and "I'm the underdog" — a single BC policy averaging across these may learn muddier behavior than three specialized heads. A mixture-of-experts pattern conditioned on threat configuration is plausible. Speculative; not part of MVP. Listed here as a thread to potentially pull on if BC plateaus.

### Replay-buffer alliance prior — for eventual PPO phase

When PPO self-play is running, keep a small fraction of every batch coming from BC trajectories. Not just for stability — the BC data contains the *only* examples of multi-agent coordination the agent will ever see during training. Without this, the agent slowly drifts away from the implicit-coordination behaviors BC taught it.

Treat the BC corpus as a strategic anchor during RL, not just an initialization.

### KL regularization back to BC during PPO handoff

Standard technique (used by AlphaStar). Prevents early-PPO gradients (noisy) from wrecking the BC initialization. Also keeps the agent in states the value head understands while the value head adapts to the shifting state distribution. Lower learning rate than from-scratch PPO; you're polishing, not learning.

## 8. Algorithmic landscape — connections to this project

Brief notes on adjacent techniques and why each is or isn't relevant. Not full explanations — those are reference material to look up separately if needed.

### Reference recipe
- **Straka & Schmid 2025** — BC + self-play PPO on 1v1 generals. Top 0.003% in 36 hours on H100. Repo (strakam/generals-bots) gives us the env (1v1) and a partial PPO scaffold (not the paper agent's training code). Closest published prior work to what we're doing.

### Stabilization techniques worth borrowing later
- **KL regularization to BC** — handoff stability for BC → PPO transition (AlphaStar). Standard now.
- **Historical-pool / fictitious self-play** — opponent diversity. Sufficient for our case (no league play needed without coordination problem).
- **DAgger-style return to BC** — re-clone the RL agent, mix BC data with on-policy data. Optional Phase 2 polish to keep agent grounded in human-like play.
- **Auxiliary heads (placement, attack-target, eliminations)** — shape trunk representation via multi-task supervised losses. Free regularizer.

### Adjacent published work
- **AlphaStar** — team play (us vs. them), not free-for-all. Population/league design less relevant than I initially thought given coordination isn't the bottleneck.
- **OpenAI Five (Dota)** — same caveat. Team coordination, not FFA.
- **Cicero / no-press Diplomacy** — closest published n-player general-sum analogs. Reference *if* coordination becomes a problem.
- **Player of Games / Student of Games (Schmid)** — principled approach for imperfect info via public belief states + GT-CFR. Beautiful theory, no clean open-source impl, ~6+ months to implement. Schmid himself reached for PPO when building generals; we follow that lead.
- **Stochastic MuZero** — handles stochastic transitions, not partial observability. Wrong tool for generals.

### Tabula-rasa methods we're explicitly not pursuing
- **AlphaZero variants** — see §2 reasoning. Many open-source implementations exist (suragnair/alpha-zero-general, OpenSpiel, mctx, kZero) if we ever want to revisit, but the structural mismatch is real.

## 9. Corrections to prior assumptions

These are common mistakes that future-me (or future-Claude in a fresh session) might re-derive incorrectly without explicit anchors.

- **Strakam env does NOT support 8-player FFA out of the box.** PettingZoo interface is a wrapper; the underlying engine is hardcoded 1v1 throughout (`(2, H, W)` ownership, enumerated 2-player branches in `game.py`). FFA support requires forking the engine.
- **The published `_experimental/ppo/` is NOT the paper agent's training code.** It's a 313-line bare-bones PPO with hardcoded random opponents, no checkpointing, no self-play. Useful as a JAX/Equinox idiom reference; not a recipe to follow. We rebuild training infra.
- **The published network drops 5 of 14 obs channels** and uses a 9-action policy head. Probably not what the paper agent uses. We design our own.
- **Engine refactor is NOT on the MVP critical path.** BC is supervised on parser output; doesn't need an FFA-aware engine. Easy to forget given the survey doc's prominence.
- **`remote/generalsio_state.py` is the highest-leverage finding from the repo survey.** Implements diff decoding for the live websocket protocol; likely matches `.gioreplay` format. Useful as parser scaffold AND on the live deployment path. Hardcoded 1v1 on line 10 (small fix).

## 10. Open questions and assumptions to validate

Pulled out as a flat list for easy review and updating. Resolved questions move to the relevant section above.

### Open questions

- **Top-k cloning policy.** Whether to clone winners only, top-3, or all 8 placements with placement-conditioned weighting. Lean is top-3 for MVP simplicity; player-conditioned cloning is richer but adds architectural complexity. Resolution depends partly on how diverse winning playstyles are in the corpus.
- **Endgame upsampling form.** A 1800-turn game has ~1800 early-game positions and ~50 endgame positions, but endgame skill (1v1 collapse) matters disproportionately. Likely fix is turn-based weighting in the dataloader — exact functional form (linear ramp? exponential weight on late game?) is TBD.
- **Per-cell observation channel design for FFA.** Extending strakam's 14 channels to handle multiple opponents. Open: per-opponent ownership masks vs. aggregated "any enemy" mask; whether to include last-seen army counts in fog and time-since-seen. Most consequential observation decision; affects parser, network, and any future env work.
- **Opponent slot count and sort key.** How many opponents to expose to the network (3? 5? all 7?), sorted by what (army count? land count? distance? learned threat score?). Sort key choice affects what the network can learn to attend to.
- **Global feature encoding.** Whether leaderboard scalars (rank, gaps, eliminated count, turn) get broadcast as constant channels, fed to a separate vector input, or both. Coupled to network architecture choice.
- **Memory mechanism.** LSTM, transformer over a window, or stacked frames for handling fog-of-war state across turns. Each has different compute, parameter count, and training stability tradeoffs.
- **Network architecture.** ResNet vs. ViT-style vs. U-Net for the spatial backbone. Lean toward ResNet for simplicity and known-good performance on game RL, but worth deliberate consideration once observation schema is settled.
- **Reward shaping function form (Phase 2).** Placement-based potential function — rank-based, exponential, or something else. Coupled to how reward transitions from placement-based to win/loss as players are eliminated.
- **Per-step shaping signals (Phase 2).** Borrow from strakam's `composite_reward_fn` (army growth, land captured, generals captured) but adapt for multi-opponent. Open: max over opponents (paranoid), mean (balanced), per-opponent terms (richest), or scrap shaping for placement-only.

### Assumptions to validate (in roughly priority order)

- **Coordination doesn't need explicit handling.** User's read of FFA dynamics suggests implicit coordination (retaliation, threat-aware aggression) is sufficient and explicit alliances are rare. If the trained agent gets visibly ganged up on by humans recognizing bot behavior, this assumption needs revisiting.
- **BC alone produces useful skill level on FFA.** The whole Phase 1 framing rests on this. If BC plateaus at clearly-mediocre play, Phase 2 (engine refactor + RL) becomes load-bearing rather than optional.
- **Post-filter replay corpus (~25-70k games) is sufficient for BC.** Per-trajectory size makes raw-game-count seem less critical than expected, but this hasn't been empirically validated for FFA specifically.
- **Parser output is correct.** Highest-leverage assumption to verify early — silently wrong observations contaminate everything downstream. End-to-end sanity checks (reconstruct known states, compare to ground truth) before committing to large training runs.
- **1v1 BC pretraining isn't worth the negative transfer cost.** Reasoning is sound but unverified. A small experiment (1v1-trained BC vs. FFA-trained BC, both evaluated on next-action prediction over FFA frames) could check this if BC underperforms expectations.
- **Single-H100-class compute is sufficient for eventual RL phase.** Strakam achieved top 0.003% in 1v1 with 36 hours on one H100. FFA is harder (more agents per game, sparser placement reward, longer credit assignment) and may need 2-5x more compute or a larger machine; unknown until tried.
- **Placement-based reward produces aggressive (not turtle) play under self-play.** Placement reward could in principle incentivize merely surviving rather than winning, leading to passive play. Form of the potential function (and transition to win/loss) matters for avoiding this; needs iteration once self-play is running.
