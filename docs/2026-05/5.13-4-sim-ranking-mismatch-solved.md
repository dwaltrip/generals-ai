# Session Notes — Surrender-Bonus `has_kill` Rule

## The rule, precisely

After v30.9.2 (deployed ~2025-11-30 05:30 UTC), the server's listings ranking partitions players by `has_kill` before the alive/death-order/army/tiles tiebreakers. The server's `has_kill` for player *p* is True iff at least one of:

**(A) Real capture.** *p* captured another player's general (the standard `execute_player_capture` path). Our sim credits this unconditionally; the sweep is 100%, so the server's behavior on the edge cases (capturing an already-AFK'd player's general) is consistent with crediting unconditionally too.

**(B) Surrender-bonus credit.** Somebody surrendered (died via the AFK-while-alive branch — *not* via general capture), and *p* is the unique player matching all of:

1. *p* dealt the most **offense-only damage** to the surrenderer — sum of `min(incoming, dest_army)` across all of *p*'s attacks on the surrenderer's tiles. Defensive damage doesn't count: army of the surrenderer that *p* destroyed by *defending* against the surrenderer's attacks is excluded.
2. Damage is summed **across the entire game**, including damage dealt during the surrenderer's AFK countdown (between the first afk and full neutralization).
3. *p* is **alive at the moment the surrenderer's tiles fully neutralize** (the 2nd afk event triggering `try_neutralize_player`) — *not* merely alive at the surrender moment. A damager who was top at surrender time but gets killed during the countdown forfeits the credit; it cascades to the next-highest-damage survivor.
4. *p* dealt strictly positive damage. If nobody qualifies (no one attacked the surrenderer, or all attackers died before neutralization), no credit is awarded.

If the game ends before the surrenderer fully neutralizes, end-of-game is used as the cutoff.

**One surrender contributes at most one credit.** Multiple credits accumulate as separate "kill" counts on the profile page; for the `has_kill` bool, once-true-stays-true.

## What this isn't

- **Not in the bundle.** The JS bundle's `lbSort` partitions on `capturedPlayers.length > 0` only — no surrender bonus. The bundle's chat showing "X captured Y" doesn't tell you whether the *server* credited that as a kill; only that a general flip happened.
- **Not pre-v30.9.2-relevant for ranking.** Pre-cutoff lbSort doesn't partition on `has_kill`, so this rule has no observable effect on ranking. The server may have been computing `has_kill` the same way all along — best guess is it was being used for star rankings behind the scenes, just not surfaced in the UI listings.
- **Not "alive at surrender".** Easy misreading of the dev's "still alive" Discord quote. Neutralization is what matters.
- **Not symmetric damage.** Tracking both attacker→defender and defender→attacker army loss was an early hypothesis. Defensive damage gets credited to the wrong player.
- **Not visible in the official replay viewer.** The generals.io replay viewer still shows the old simple ordering (without surrender bonuses) — apparently a real bug in their viewer, but not game-breaking since it's an auxiliary post-game tool. This was the source of considerable initial confusion: the listings (which use the server's ranking) and the replay viewer (which uses the bundle's lbSort) disagree, and only the listings reflect what the game actually decided.

# The investigation

## Where we started

Previous session: pre-v30.9.2 ranking match 100%, post-v30.9.2 stuck at ~92%. Mismatches localized to `has_kill` since the kill/no-kill partition was the only ranking change in v30.9.2. Three vague hypotheses: capture-by-surrender, captures-during-AFK-countdown, surrender + last-attacker credit.

## Tooling and ground-truth sources

Listed roughly in order of leverage:

1. **The official generals.io replay viewer.** The primary investigation instrument throughout. Used at every stage to step through frames and see what each player was actually doing — who attacked whom, when surrenders happened, what the countdown looked like. None of the bot-side tooling substituted for "watch the game". The viewer's own ranking display is buggy (see above), so it was used for frame-by-frame state, not as a ground-truth oracle.

2. **The profile-page UI on generals.io.** Used throughout for both the server's ranking *and* the per-player kill counts. Listings tell you the ordering; kill counts tell you exactly who has `has_kill` and by how much. Kill counts were the consistently-decisive ground-truth source whenever a listings interpretation was ambiguous.

3. **`scripts/debug_rankings.py`.** CLI taking replay IDs, printing listings-vs-deduced ranking side-by-side with player names. The per-replay drill-down tool. Iterated several times during the investigation:
   - Started bare (just listings vs deduced).
   - Added the full N×N damage matrix.
   - Filtered the matrix columns to surrenderers only (much easier to read).
   - Added the "top damager per surrenderer × variant" summary table.
   - Added a second row per surrenderer showing the alive-at-surrender filter.
   - Added the ☠ marker on players holding `has_kill` in the effective ranking.

4. **Four-variant damage matrices in `State`.** `damage_{sym|off}_{all|pre}`, all accumulated per attack. Cheap to maintain in parallel, made hypothesis comparison instant.

5. **`NeutralizeEvent` tracking** added late, after the alive-at-neutralization refinement landed.

6. **`scripts/sweep_match_rates.py`.** The closing-argument tool. Weekly-bucketed match rates across thousands of replays; tells you when you're actually done.

## The hypothesis path

**H1 — "Bug in `execute_player_capture`?"** First instinct on `Rulw-A8t2`: maybe we credit `has_kill[captor]` even when the captured player was already AFK'd, and that's wrong. Daniel checked: our logic actually matched the *bundle* correctly — the bundle credits unconditionally too. The question wasn't "is our code buggy" but "does the bundle match the server", and Daniel was already half-aware from prior Discord reading that surrender-credits-as-kills might be the missing piece. Skipped past quickly.

**H2 — Dev's Discord quote.** Daniel pulled up the dev's description: "If a player surrenders, whoever did the most damage to them gets the kill." Right shape, but the literal reading didn't quite match the data.

**H3 — "Top damager among living no-kill players."** First confusion: the raw damage matrix didn't produce the bumps we needed to match the listings — the literal top damager for some surrenders was already dead, or was the winner who already had a kill, so awarding it didn't move the right players into the kill group. I synthesized H3 (filter to alive damagers with no existing kill) as a way to explain `Rulw-A8t2` mechanically. Daniel pushed back: overfits one replay and contradicts the dev quote's "even if they already had a kill". Rejected.

**H4 — "Top offense-only damager, period."** Daniel's reading after I refactored damage tracking into 4 variants and surfaced the per-surrenderer top-damager table. Fit replays 1–4 of a 4-replay batch. I missed that `Rulw-A8t2` itself contradicts it: `off/all` picks sadww (76) for user181012's surrender, but profile shows sadww=0 kills. The dead-damager case wasn't represented in replays 1–4 because top damagers there happened to also be alive.

**H5 — "Top offense-only damager, alive at surrender."** Synthesis from H4 + the contradicting `Rulw-A8t2` data. Sweep went 92% → ~97-98%. Residual 2-3% scattered across all post-cutoff weeks.

**H6 — "Alive at *neutralization*, not surrender."** Daniel's diagnosis of three residual mismatches (`wYwi8jhTf`, `PnwwaaR8Y`, `niXEngO1A`). Each case: top damager was alive at surrender moment but got killed during the surrenderer's AFK countdown. Sweep went to **100% across all 58 weekly buckets, 2900 sampled replays**.

## Turning points

A few moments where the investigation pivoted hard:

1. **Treating listings *and* kill counts as separate ground truths.** Listings ordering alone is ambiguous — `Rulw-A8t2`'s observed order is consistent with both `{kill: sldhgas}` and `{kill: sldhgas, user181012, lagrange12}`, because dead-by-death-order tiebreaks land in the same positions either way. The profile page's per-player kill counts disambiguate immediately. Daniel had been cross-referencing both throughout; the kill-count screenshot Daniel shared mid-session was a "let me prove this to Claude" moment, not a new discovery.

2. **Filtering the damage table to surrender columns.** Going from full N×N to just the surrenderer columns made the relevant patterns immediately visible. Cosmetic, but high leverage.

3. **The "alive" filter as a second row per surrenderer.** Side-by-side display of unfiltered vs alive-filtered top damager surfaced the dead-top-damager case that H4 had quietly missed.

4. **The neutralize-vs-surrender refinement.** Required walking through individual countdowns in the replay viewer to see top-damager-gets-killed-mid-countdown — the kind of insight that comes from frame-stepping through actual games, not from the aggregate tooling.

## Methodology takeaways

- **The official replay viewer is irreplaceable.** No amount of CLI tooling substitutes for watching the game frame-by-frame to understand what's actually happening.
- **Don't trust the bundle as ground truth for derived state.** The bundle is a client-side reconstruction; server-tracked values like `has_kill` can diverge. The official replay viewer inherits the bundle's ranking, so it inherits the bug too. Server listings + profile-page kill counts are the only authoritative signal.
- **Listings ordering alone underdetermines `has_kill`.** Multiple valid `has_kill` assignments produce the same order. Per-player kill counts are needed to pin it down.
- **Track variants in parallel when the rule is unsettled.** Cheap to compute, instant hypothesis comparison. The 4-variant damage matrix paid for itself within the same session.
- **Quotes from devs are clues, not specs.** "Still alive" looked like a tiebreaker hint; turned out to be the load-bearing rule, *and* more precise than literal "alive at surrender".
- **Sweep is the closing argument.** Individual-replay hypothesizing risks overfitting. The weekly-bucketed sweep going from 92% → 97% → 100% is what tells you you're done.

## Still uncertain

A handful of edge cases the 2900-sample sweep doesn't surface but should be flagged:

1. **Multi-damager ties.** Untested edge case in the server's tiebreaker. Our `argmax` picks first slot. Likely incredibly rare in practice — if any cases exist, the full corpus sweep will surface them.
2. **Game-end-before-neutralize.** We fall back to end-of-game timestep. Untested edge case; might never happen in clean FFA games.
3. **Pre-v30.9.2 `has_kill` server-side semantics.** Best guess: server was computing this rule all along for star rankings, just not using it for the UI listings. Not directly verifiable from listings alone.
4. **`execute_player_capture` on already-dead players.** Our code credits `has_kill[captor]` even when the captured player was already AFK'd. The sweep is 100%, so the server likely matches — but if a future change ever breaks the post-cutoff sweep, this is worth re-examining.

## Implications for training?

**Daniel's brainstorm added after the session.** Should discuss with Claude at some point before we are doing full training runs.

These surrender-bonus rules are a bit quirky. In my opinion they aren't balanced properly. There's a lot of wierd edge cases or situations which result in outcomes I consider *strange* (and somewhat unreasonable). I don't think it's easy to design a perfect rule here, but I would definitely make tweaks to the rule, if I were in charge.

Previously, we've discussed adding other heads to our neural net beyond the main heads for "learning the next move to make". Things like having the model try to predict who will die next or placement outcomes. I think the theory here is that the models richer feedback this way as opposed to just having the more sparse "win / lose" signal. Strong players in our corpus win much more frequently than random (20-40% vs. 12.5%), but having adiditional high-quality signals could help, from my limited understanding.

However, given my concerns about the reasonableness of the surrender-bonus rule the way it's currently implemented on the offical site, I'm wondering if we actually want to naively use "placement outcome" as an addtional signal. It may not be a huge issue, but something to consider.
