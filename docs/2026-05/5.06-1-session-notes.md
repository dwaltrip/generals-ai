## 2. At a glance

### Decisions made

- **Contraction depth = 2 (tentative).** Documented in `network-architecture-design.md` §3.1 with RF math. Sensitivity sweep recommended.
- **Value head = categorical placement distribution (tentative).** 8-way softmax over final placement; binary win/loss as fallback. Documented in `network-architecture-design.md` §3.2.
- **Distance-from-known-generals channels added (locked for v1).** 8 channels (self + 7 opponents), BFS distance, log-scaled, sentinel for unreachable / unrevealed. Documented in `observation-tensor-design.md` §2.1, with rationale in `distance-features-design-space.md` §6.
- **Defaults inherited (pending review):** inner widths (256/320), normalization (LayerNorm), heads-as-Pyramid-Modules. Documented in `network-architecture-design.md` §6.

### Key insights / reframings

- **Strakam-existence-proof sharpened.** Earlier mental model leaned on "DeepNash → us is ~10⁴× compute below" — that was the wrong comparison. The relevant one is **Strakam → us**, which is ~36 H100-hours vs. our 30–60 H100-hours per BC run — parity, not orders of magnitude below. Strakam achieved top-25 1v1 ladder with the inherited Pyramid Module shape at our compute scale. Strong existence proof; shifts inner-width concern from "must investigate" to "default to inherited, sensitivity-sweep later." (Caveat: Strakam paper is genuinely vague on widths — existence proof is qualitative on shape, not quantitative on widths.)

- **Failure modes are quality bugs, not viability bugs.** Strakam shipped with mountain dead-ends and mode-locking and still hit top-25 1v1. The user's target bar (top 50–100 FFA, "would be incredible" as new ground) is lower than Strakam's, so addressing these failure modes is "would help" rather than "must address." This reshaped the framing of the contraction-depth decision: 2 contractions becomes a **principled but optional** improvement rather than a viability requirement.

- **CNN graph-reasoning limitation is deeper than receptive field.** Even with infinite RF, a finite-depth CNN approximates BFS poorly because each conv layer can extend reachability propagation by exactly one step. RF and graph reasoning are **separable concerns that compound** — both contribute to dead-end pathology, and addressing only RF leaves the BFS limitation. Distance-feature channels (pre-computed BFS as input) directly target the second limitation.

- **Channel utilization concern softened.** Earlier I'd estimated "15–30 of 75 channels effectively used" for the densest distance-feature scheme. That was too pessimistic. The first conv layer absorbs correlated inputs via implicit linear projection — redundancy is compressed, not wasted. The cost of redundant channels is a few thousand extra first-layer weights, not "70% wasted." Truly null channels (sentinel-dominated) remain a real but smaller concern. Net update: the user's intuition that the network would learn to use most of the channels was probably closer to right than my initial estimate.

- **Block-rep middle ground emerged as a genuinely new framing.** During the design-space discussion, the design space initially looked like "curated point-source vs. dense block-region." A middle scheme — one representative cell per block, fixed-grid systematic coverage, ~25 channels — emerged in the discussion and dominated the dense block-region scheme on cost-per-information for a wide range of cases. Now preserved in `distance-features-design-space.md` §4.2 as a juicy thread.

- **User's win-rate context updated the value-head argument.** My initial "binary value head is too sparse for FFA" framing used the uniform-skill 12.5% positive rate. Strong-player corpus rates are 25–40% (top players 40+%), making binary much less catastrophic. Categorical placement is still the better choice (higher signal density, multimodality capture, PPO compatibility), but binary is a viable fallback rather than a dead end.

## 3. Per-topic discussion (compressed)

Most substantive material lives in topical docs. This section captures the discussion arc and what shifted in our thinking.

### 3.1 Architecture priority list (where we started, what landed)

Last session had identified an architecture priority list:

1. Contraction depth
2. Value head output shape
3. Width
4. Normalization
5. Heads-as-Pyramid-Modules

This session worked the list top-down. Items 1 and 2 got real treatment and tentative decisions. Items 3–5 got "default to inherited" calls — not because they're unimportant, but because the existence-proof sharpening (§2 above) plus the compute reframe (last session) reduced the urgency of investigating them for v1.

The framing reframed during the session: **early in the session I was leaning hard on compute pessimism ("must shrink for our budget") and on FFA-makes-everything-harder ("must address mountain dead-ends").** The user pushed back on both — Strakam's existence proof undercuts the compute pessimism, and the user's target bar makes the failure modes "quality" rather than "viability." Both pushbacks landed and reshaped the rest of the session.

### 3.2 Contraction depth deep-dive

The substantive work for this decision is in `network-architecture-design.md` §3.1. Highlights of the discussion:

- Walked through the receptive-field math layer by layer for DeepNash's torso. Theoretical RF ≈ 33 at the bottleneck, ~3× coverage of 10×10. Same architecture on 25×25: RF still 33 (architecture-only), barely larger than board side, just under diagonal (~35.4).
- Effective receptive field caveat (Luo et al. 2017): typically Gaussian-shaped, much smaller than theoretical. Compounds the concern.
- Two contractions on 25×25: RF ≈ 77, restoring DeepNash's design intent of ~3× coverage.
- Compute math worked through carefully. Per-sample FLOPs increase ~2–10%, params increase ~50%. Both well within budget.

User noted they'd want to verify the CNN/RF specifics later (outside their current expertise level) — the decision is tentative-locked with an explicit invitation to revisit.

### 3.3 Value head output shape

Substantive content in `network-architecture-design.md` §3.2. Discussion arc:

- Initial framing: scalar binary win/loss is "too sparse for FFA" given uniform-skill 12.5% positive class.
- Categorical placement distribution: ~3 bits/sample vs. 1 bit, captures multimodality, distributional value-head literature (C51 etc.) shows representation-level advantages.
- User correction: strong-player corpus has 25–40% win rates (top players 40+%). Binary is much less catastrophic at that balance.
- Landed: categorical placement primary, binary as held-in-reserve fallback.

### 3.4 Distance features design space (productive tangent)

What started as "are there CNN tricks for grid distances?" became a substantive design-space exploration. Material consolidated in `distance-features-design-space.md`. Discussion arc highlights:

- Established that the actual problem is **graph reasoning, not just receptive field**. CNNs approximate BFS poorly even with global RF.
- Pre-computed distance channels are the cleanest entry-point mitigation.
- User commented that this is a wide design space, and as a demonstration, proposed a hierarchical block-region scheme (~75 channels, multi-region per block).
- I initially critiqued it sharply; user pushed back well on identity-stability, elongated-region, and edge-case concerns. **Most of those critiques were overstated or wrong** (notably the boundary-artifact one — block-regions are by-construction limited to single blocks, which I'd misunderstood).
- The cleaner remaining concern (sparse channels at our scale) was also softer than I'd framed once we accounted for first-layer linear-projection compression.
- The block-rep middle ground emerged as a genuinely new framing during the discussion — same hierarchical instinct as the user's scheme, much cheaper engineering.
- V1 decision: minimal point-source set (BFS to each general). The richer schemes were preserved for future iteration, along with the insight that there many design levers and much room for creativity to efficiently mitigate this large known limitation of CNNs.
