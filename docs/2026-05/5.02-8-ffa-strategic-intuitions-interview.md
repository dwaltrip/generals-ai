# FFA Strategic Intuitions: Interview Notes

**Date:** 2026.05.02
**Source:** Conversational interview with the user, who plays FFA at high rank on the live ladder.
**Purpose:** Capture how a strong human FFA player actually thinks about the game state — what they track, what inferences they make, how their attention shifts across game phases. Reference doc for future architectural iteration; deliberately separated from architecture decisions to keep the strategic thinking clean.

The interview was conducted one question at a time and captured incrementally. This doc reorganizes the answers into a coherent picture of strategic intuition, with the original phrasing preserved where helpful.

---

## 1. Attention model: who am I tracking?

**The fundamental gating signal is contact.** "The most important thing to track is the players I have come into contact with — e.g., my tiles have vision of at least 1 of their tiles." Players you haven't contacted yet are effectively background — present on the leaderboard but not part of active strategic reasoning.

**Attention scales with game phase:**

- **Early game:** "Most of the time, you can ignore anyone you haven't seen yet." Active attention is concentrated on contacted opponents only. The leaderboard exists in the background but doesn't drive moment-to-moment decisions.
- **Mid-game (3-5 players remaining):** "You need to keep a regular eye on the entire leaderboard." Attention broadens. Even uncontacted players become relevant because the strategic situation is condensing.
- **Late game (last few players):** Implicit in the discussion but not stated explicitly — attention concentrates further onto specific surviving opponents and the geographic/strategic structure of the endgame.

**Implication:** "tracking" is not uniform. There's an active set of opponents being actively modeled (working theory of what they're doing) and a passive set being monitored (occasional leaderboard checks for surprises). The active set is roughly 1-3 in early game, expanding to most of the field by mid-game.

## 2. Per-opponent state being tracked

For each opponent under active attention, the player tracks several things in parallel:

### 2.1 Relative strength compared to the rest of the field

This is the highest-level read on each player. Not absolute army or land counts in isolation — the question is "where does this player sit relative to everyone else." Underspecified in the interview but importantly *relational*: a player with 200 army is strong if everyone else has 100, weak if everyone else has 400.

### 2.2 City count

Inferred from a specific signal: army growth between consecutive turns when the player isn't actively fighting. "You can check this by army count growth between 2 sequential turns where they aren't actively fighting (e.g., army goes from 120 → 122 and then 122 → 124: this means they have 1 city in addition to the general)."

The general produces +1 army per turn during peacetime, plus +1 per city. So observed peacetime growth rate, minus 1 (for the general), gives city count. This is a **policy-independent inference** from publicly available information — derivable from the scoreboard alone, doesn't require modeling the opponent.

City count is meaningful because it's a leading indicator of future army production. A player with 3 cities will outscale a player with 1 city given equal land.

### 2.3 Land count direction (growing vs. shrinking)

"Is the land count growing or shrinking? If it's growing consistently over a series of turns, they are probably attacking. If it's shrinking they are by definition being attacked."

The directional read is itself useful (attacker vs. attacked), but it also enables a cross-player inference described next.

### 2.4 Cross-player conflict pattern

"If another player's land is shrinking / growing at a similar rate, then they are probably the other side of the coin: the attacker or defender for the player we are analyzing."

So watching land-count-deltas across multiple players lets you infer **who is fighting whom**, even for players you have no direct vision of. If player A is shrinking by ~5 land/turn and player B is growing by ~5 land/turn, that's evidence A and B are in a conflict — without ever seeing the contested territory.

This is one of the more interesting inferences in the interview. It's a cross-referenced derivation: a single player's land-direction signal isn't fully informative on its own, but the whole set of signals together reveals conflict structure invisible behind fog.

## 3. Threat assessment: "How likely is a player to attack me?"

This is one of the core strategic questions, and it integrates several inputs:

- **Proximity to your border.** Closer = higher threat.
- **Army size.** Bigger army = more capacity to threaten.
- **Whether they're currently fighting someone else.** Fighting elsewhere = lower immediate threat to me.
- **Their position overall.** Strong vs. weak.
- **History of recent aggression toward me specifically.** Past behavior is signal.

A specific concrete signal: **observed enemy units moving past or away from your position** are typically attacking someone else. "If I see a larger army for that color move past or away from my position, that usually means they are attacking someone else, especially early game."

This is a movement-direction read. The mere presence of a large enemy army doesn't tell you they're targeting you; the *trajectory* of that army does.

**Important subtlety:** this is a move-history-derived inference, not a static-state inference. You have to integrate over recent observed movement to extract direction. A snapshot of the current state doesn't capture it.

## 4. Self-assessment: "How safe am I? Should I consolidate or be aggressive?"

The mirror question of threat assessment. The player tracks several things to answer:

### 4.1 Own metrics

- **Own army and land size.** Both in absolute terms and relative to the field.

### 4.2 General-area defensibility

- **Buffer around the general.** "How much of a buffer do I have around my general (harder for them to find me as they have to go through more of my land, and more time for me to react and defend)."

This is geographic, not just metric. The amount of *owned space between the enemy's likely entry vector and your general* matters. A player with 100 land all clumped near their general is more vulnerable than a player with 200 land spread out, even though the latter has more total land.

The buffer serves dual purposes: hides the general (they have to search more land to find it) AND gives reaction time (more turns between when they cross your border and when they reach the general).

### 4.3 Unexplored regions and surprise threats

- **Unexplored pockets nearby.** "Are there any unexplored pockets nearby where another player might be? Who could attack me out of the blue?"
- "Especially if I have seen all players but 1 or 2."

The number of opponents you've made contact with vs. total alive is itself a signal. If 6 of 7 opponents are contacted, the 7th is somewhere — the question is where, and whether they're positioned to surprise you.

This combines geography (where are unexplored regions) with meta-state (how many uncontacted opponents exist). Together they tell you how much surprise risk you're carrying.

### 4.4 Field-wide conflict state

- **Are other players fighting?** When other players are tied up, you're less exposed; when the field is calm, more eyes are looking for opportunities.

This is the same cross-player conflict inference from §2.4, applied to self-assessment instead of opponent-modeling.

## 5. Higher-order strategic patterns

### 5.1 Game-shape recognition

In the 4-player phase, a specific structural pattern emerges: "you might notice that 2 players seem to be fighting, and then realize you need to find the 4th player, as they are likely growing uncontested / planning an assault of their own (especially if they realize the other 1v1 is going on)."

This is a **structural recognition** of "two parallel 1v1s plus an uncontested fourth." The inference chain:
1. Observe that 2 specific players are fighting (via the cross-player conflict pattern).
2. Realize you and the remaining 4th player are not in that fight.
3. Conclude the 4th player has time/space to grow uncontested.
4. Conclude you should also be using this time, OR finding the 4th player before they find you.

This pattern emerges naturally from geography and exploration rather than being something the player actively engineers. "It often happens naturally for geographic / exploration reasons."

### 5.2 Endgame transitions

When the field condenses to 4, common path is two parallel 1v1s resolving to a final 1v1. When the field condenses further, FFA effectively becomes 1v1. Implicit in the interview: late-game strategic skill *is* 1v1 strategic skill, applied to whoever survives. The transition from FFA-mode to 1v1-mode happens organically as players are eliminated.

### 5.3 Scaling of attention with player count

The implicit claim is that strategic complexity decreases as players are eliminated, but the *attention burden* doesn't decrease linearly:

- 8 players, no contact: low burden (most players are background).
- 8 players, lots of contact: high burden (multiple active opponents to model).
- 4 players, all in conflict: high burden (whole field is active).
- 2 players: focused but deep (full 1v1 strategic space).

Mid-game (3-5 players, all contacted, all alive) is plausibly the highest-attention phase.

## 6. The role of the leaderboard / scoreboard

Several inferences in this doc depend on the publicly-available scoreboard (per-player land totals and army totals, updated each turn):

- City count inference (§2.2) — derived from peacetime army-growth rate.
- Conflict-detection (§2.4) — derived from land-count-delta correlations across players.
- Cross-checking visual observations against scoreboard reality (e.g., "I see them attacking me, and the scoreboard confirms my land is dropping").
- Tracking uncontacted players you can't see directly — they're still on the leaderboard, so their growth/strength is still visible.

The scoreboard is **the primary information channel for opponents you have no visual contact with**, and a key cross-check for opponents you do have contact with.

The interview implicitly treats the scoreboard as an always-on background signal that's actively read, not just glanced at occasionally. Strong players are continuously processing scoreboard changes turn-by-turn, especially in mid-game.

## 7. Information about myself that opponents can see

Implicit but worth surfacing: the scoreboard cuts both ways. Everything I'm inferring about opponents from the scoreboard, they can infer about me. This means:

- My city count is inferable from my army growth.
- My current conflicts are inferable from my land-count direction.
- My relative strength is broadcast to everyone.

A strong player presumably reasons about this: "if my army is growing fast, opponents know I have cities and I'm becoming a threat — they might gang up on me." This kind of meta-reasoning didn't come up explicitly in the interview but seems likely to be present in expert play.

## 8. Patterns across all the above

A few cross-cutting observations on how the strategic intuitions are structured:

### Most inferences are cross-referencing, not single-signal

Almost nothing in this doc is "look at one number and decide." Threat assessment combines proximity + army size + their other commitments + history. Conflict detection combines deltas across multiple players. Self-assessment combines own metrics + geography + meta-state about uncontacted opponents. The strong-player reasoning seems to be heavily about *correlating multiple signals* rather than reading any single one.

### Geography matters as much as metrics

The buffer-around-general read, the unexplored-pocket read, the spatial structure of "two parallel 1v1s" — these are all geographic intuitions. The metrics (army count, land count) are necessary but not sufficient. Two players with identical scoreboard stats can be in very different situations depending on the spatial layout.

### Time-derivative signals matter as much as snapshots

City count comes from growth rate, not current army. Conflict detection comes from delta correlations. Movement-direction comes from observed trajectories. The strong-player reasoning is heavily about *change over time*, not just current state. A still-frame of the board is much less informative than a few turns of movement.

### Inference fills in for what you can't directly observe

The scoreboard-derived inferences (city count, conflict pairings) are doing the work of *converting public information into approximate private information*. You can't see opponent cities directly behind fog, but you can derive city count from public growth rate. You can't see opponent battles you're not part of, but you can infer them from public land-delta patterns.

This is the part of strong play that distinguishes it most sharply from naive play: extracting maximum information from the public scoreboard rather than treating it as just a leaderboard.

### Attention is dynamic

The active-opponent set, the passive-opponent set, the geography being attended to, the inference depth being applied — all of these shift with game phase, with own situation, with observed events. Strong play involves moment-to-moment reallocation of attention, not a fixed routine.
