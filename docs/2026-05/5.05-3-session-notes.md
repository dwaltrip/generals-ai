## 2. At a glance

### Decisions made

- **Slot ordering**: canonical-by-ID at runtime + random slot-permutation augmentation in the training dataloader. Supersedes the implicit "canonical-by-ID alone" choice in 5.03-3 §3.2 by adding the augmentation layer.
- **Drop `passable` channel** from the obs tensor spec. The 5.05-1 §A inclusion was inconsistent with §7.4's own observation that `passable` is derivable from `mountains`.
- **Phase 1 evaluation prioritized on output-move quality** (user-clarified priority). Live deployment + inference latency are explicitly de-prioritized for now. This shapes downstream design discussions: latency stops being a binding constraint; the model "working" means output-move quality, not ladder strength.

### Key insights / reframings

- **Channel count is not the binding compute lever.** First-conv FLOPS are 1–3% of total; channel-count savings are correspondingly small. The actually-binding levers are inner pyramid width, spatial extent, and dataset × perspectives × turns. The cost of more channels lives in *engineering complexity* and *inference latency*, not training compute. Detail in `compute-considerations.md` §5.
- **But channel count still has an architectural cost — it just isn't compute.** Sparse-input channels (especially `last_seen_owner`, `opp_N_contacted`, per-timestep dense history) carry small per-channel gradient signal and risk being *underutilized* by the network even when they're free compute-wise. "Channels don't burn FLOPS" doesn't mean channel count is unconstrained — it shifts the binding cost from compute to *learnability* (sample efficiency per input pathway, gradient dilution, U-Net bottleneck capacity utilization). Where the line falls is empirical, not derivable from theory. Discussed at the end of §3.1 as the complement to the compute reframe.
- **Two kinds of "consistency" for slot ordering** — within-game (canonical-by-ID gives) and across-game (sort-by-threat gives). They are orthogonal types. The doc treatment in 5.03-3 §3.2 chose the first without naming the second; the right answer turns out to be "get both via augmentation."
- **Slot-permutation augmentation is the third option** in the slot-ordering tradespace, and dominates both canonical-by-ID-alone and dynamic-sort-with-mitigations on cost-to-benefit. Almost free, no architecture change, no extra channels, no parser complexity.
- **BC compute headroom is bigger than initially framed.** Strakam's 36 H100-hours was ~3 hours BC + ~33 hours self-play. Phase 1 BC alone has ~30–60 H100-hours of headroom on hobby budget — substantially more breathing room than treating Strakam's full 36-hour figure as the BC budget would imply.
- **User-as-evaluator (top 25–50 FFA player) is a major asset** most ML projects don't have. It enables move-review evaluation (high information per hour, no engine needed) as a viable evaluation channel without waiting for a deployable bot.
- **Filter design > more scraping** at this dataset scale. The current ~80k filtered corpus is already past the "enough samples for BC" line. The leverage from more raw data is filter restrictiveness, not raw sample volume.

## 3. Per-topic discussion

### 3.1 Compute reframe (where the session started, where it landed)

The session opened by flagging "channel count vs. compute budget hasn't been quantified" as the most important under-examined claim in the obs-tensor docs. The 5.05-1 framing — "we're at the same scale as DeepNash's 82 channels, expected for FFA" — was glossing over an important asymmetry: DeepNash trained on a ~10×10 board with ~4,000 TPU v3 chips for an unspecified but presumably multi-week duration. We're targeting ~25×25 boards on a single H100 with hobby-budget cloud rental.

The investigation went through several stages:

1. **Extract real numbers from DeepNash.** A subagent was tasked with extracting compute-specific claims from the paper. It found 768 + 256 = 1,024 "TPU nodes" (paper §42), 7.21M learner steps × 768-trajectory batch (paper §32), but no wall-clock duration, no TPU generation, no parameter count. The Sebulba convention (per the architecture paper DeepNash cites) is that "node" = host of 4 chips, so 1,024 nodes is most likely ~4,096 chips total.
2. **TPU↔H100 conversion.** Peak BF16 TFLOPS: TPU v3 ~123, TPU v4 ~275, H100 ~990. So 1 H100 ≈ 4–8 TPU chips depending on generation.
3. **Baseline TPU v3.** Per the user's `deepnash-tpu-analysis.md` (~80% confidence based on contemporary DeepMind papers, project timeline vs. v4 availability, non-flagship status, and the paper's silence on the platform).
4. **Compute asymmetry estimate.** ~4,096 v3 chips ≈ ~510 H100-equivalents of peak compute. Multiplied by an unspecified-but-presumably-multi-week wall clock, the order-of-magnitude estimate is **~10⁴× Strakam's 36 H100-hours**. We're inheriting an architecture that was tuned at a compute scale 10,000× ours.
5. **Apple Silicon viability check.** M1 Max GPU ~22 TFLOPS FP16, M1 Ultra ~42 TFLOPS — so 25–45× slower than 1 H100. A full BC run would take 30–110 days, theoretically feasible but with a brutal iteration loop. Recommended split: local M1 for development, cloud H100 for runs that need to count.
6. **BC compute estimate.** ~80k raw → ~40–56k filtered → ~55–80k usable trajectories → ~100–140M training samples. ~30–60 H100-hours per BC run, ~$60–600 cloud rental. 5–10 iteration runs comfortably within hobby budget.
7. **The actual reframe.** A back-of-envelope on per-sample FLOPS showed that the first conv layer is only 1–3% of total network FLOPS. The U-Net's compute is dominated by inner pyramid layers operating at fixed 256/320 channels regardless of input. Cutting one input channel saves ~0.01–0.03% of total compute. The 9-channel one-hot block saves 0.1–0.3%. Halving N saves ~1–2%. **Channel count isn't the lever we thought it was.** The actually-binding levers are inner pyramid width, spatial extent, and dataset × perspectives × turns.

This is the most consequential output of the session for future architectural discussions: questions about the obs tensor should reframe from "is this affordable?" to "is this engineering complexity worth it?" — those are different cost models with different answers.

All of this material is consolidated in `compute-considerations.md`. The session-specific context above explains how the reframe was arrived at; the topical doc should be the reference for ongoing work.

#### Complementary architectural reframe: channels still have to earn their keep

Late in the session, the user surfaced a related but distinct concern: even if channel count is cheap compute-wise, that doesn't mean it's *architecturally* free. The costs just live elsewhere — in **learnability** rather than FLOPS.

Four separable failure modes for too-many-channels at our scale:

1. **Sample efficiency per input pathway.** Every channel adds parameters in the first conv layer. *Dense* channels (binary masks updated every frame, broadcast scalars) get plenty of training signal — every sample carries information for those weights. *Sparse* channels (capture events that occur ~7 times per game, `last_seen_owner` cells the agent rarely visits) have a much smaller effective sample count. The network may simply not learn to use them.
2. **Gradient signal dilution.** Each input pathway gets a fraction of the gradient. With many parallel channels, signal per channel is smaller; combined with sparse activations, some channels can effectively never converge.
3. **Capacity utilization through the U-Net.** The pyramid bottleneck has a fixed information capacity. Channels past what the bottleneck can absorb are at best wasted — the network compresses by learning to ignore the ones that don't earn their keep.
4. **Spurious correlation surface.** More inputs = more potential shortcuts the network might latch onto in the training distribution. Real concern but probably the smallest of the four at our sample scale.

Concretely affects the same channels we'd already flagged for other reasons:

- **The 9-channel `last_seen_owner` one-hot** — already flagged for spatial-pattern reasons (shells vs blobs). Compounded by sparse-channel signal dilution: ~1–2% per-channel non-zero rate, distributed across 9 parallel pathways. Plausible failure mode: network learns to use 1–2 of them and treats the rest as noise.
- **The 7 `opp_N_contacted` broadcast scalars** — extremely sparse signal (binary, 0 most of the game, 1 once contacted). Could plausibly carry the same information in a denser form.
- **Dense history at N=10** (20 channels) — most cells are zero per timestep; diminishing returns past some N.

Crucially, **where the "too many channels" line falls for our specific configuration is empirical, not derivable from theory.** Vision and multi-modal networks routinely train fine with thousands of input channels — but on much larger datasets and at much larger model sizes. At our scale, "what fraction of channels does the network actually learn to use" is the right question, and the only way to answer it is to train and inspect.

Two implications for ongoing work:

1. **Adds weight to the `last_seen_owner` empirical check** (already in deferred items §2). The check was originally framed around spatial-pattern detection; the learnability concern compounds it. Both lines of reasoning motivate the same investigation.
2. **Suggests a v1 bias toward parsimony.** Easier to add channels later, once the ones you have are confirmed pulling weight, than to discover after the fact that several were dead weight.

A short list of architectural mitigations exists if channel utilization turns out to be a problem (per-channel learnable gating, input normalization, channel grouping in the input projection, auxiliary reconstruction losses). All deferred — this section is intentionally framing-and-problem; mitigations get tackled later if the problem materializes.

### 3.2 Phase 1 priorities clarification (load-bearing context for everything else)

The user clarified mid-session that the high-level priority for Phase 1 is **building a bot that "works"** — judged primarily on the quality of its output moves. Live deployment, robust ladder play, and short inference time are explicitly lower priority for now. The user is willing to iterate and optimize for deployment later if the bot turns out to be worth deploying.

This clarification reshapes several discussions:

- **Inference latency stops being a binding constraint.** Channel count, network depth, network width — none of these need to be sized for sub-500ms inference on consumer hardware. The compute reframe in §3.1 took on its full implication only after this clarification: with deployment de-prioritized, "channel count = engineering complexity" is the only remaining question, and engineering complexity is a much softer constraint than per-inference compute.
- **The "will the model learn" question crowds out the "can it be deployed" question.** Most of the open risks for Phase 1 viability are now about whether the model can learn the right thing from the data, not whether the trained model can be deployed cheaply. This is where the obs-tensor bottleneck risks (slot ordering, sparse channels) become the load-bearing concerns.
- **Hobby-budget compute is genuinely sufficient.** Cloud H100 rental at $2–10/hr × 30–60 hours per BC run = $60–600 per training run, well within hobby budget for 5–10 iterations.
- **Evaluation methodology becomes the next-most-important undefined piece.** "Performs well on output moves" is the criterion, but the concrete metric isn't pinned down. (Discussed below in §3.4; pinned for a later session as the standard-methodologies walkthrough.)

### 3.3 Slot ordering — the deep dive (locked decision)

This was the most substantive technical discussion of the session and produced the locked-in decision: **canonical-by-ID at runtime + random slot-permutation augmentation in the training dataloader.**

#### The contradiction

The starting observation: 5.02-3 §5 commits to *sort-by-threat* opponent ordering — explicitly so the network never sees "player 3" but always sees "biggest threat in slot 0." The whole point is permutation invariance: lets the network generalize across all 8 perspectives, treats slots as interchangeable roles rather than fixed identities.

5.03-3 §3.2 reverses this to *canonical-by-ID* (opp-N is always the same player throughout the game) without naming the prior decision being overridden. The reasoning in 5.03-3 is real: a mid-game sort-swap (e.g., opponent rankings shift) would corrupt all spatial + history channels for the swapped slots. A tile labeled "opp_2's territory" would suddenly belong to a different opponent, and the network has no way to know.

#### Two kinds of consistency (the framing that resolved it)

The framing introduced this session: "slot mapping needs to be consistent" can mean two different things, and they are *orthogonal*:

- **Within-game consistency**: within a single game, slot N always refers to the same actual player. Canonical-by-ID gives you this. Critical for history channels: `ownership_transition[t-5]`'s "opp 0" needs to refer to the same player as the current "opp 0," or the channels disagree with each other.
- **Across-game consistency**: across different games, slot N always refers to the same *role*. Sort-by-army gives you this (slot 0 = "biggest army opponent" in every game). Critical for generalizable patterns: the network learning "what to do when the biggest opponent does X" only works if "biggest opponent" is in a known place.

Canonical-by-ID gets you within-game consistency, loses across-game. Sort-by-threat gets you across-game, loses within-game (because the sort changes mid-game, corrupting history). The 5.03-3 reasoning correctly identified the within-game cost of sort-by-threat but never named the across-game cost it was implicitly accepting.

#### The mitigation tradespace

Three strategies for resolving the tension were considered:

**(A) Dynamic sort + re-permute history channels each timestep.** No extra channels, but the parser becomes a state machine: every timestep, compute the current sort order, look at the stored history (under various past orderings), re-permute it to match. This is a real engineering cost: the parser is already the highest-risk component of the project (per 5.02-3 §3), and adding state-machine re-permutation logic compounds that risk. From the model's perspective, this fully solves the problem: because the model is *stateless across inference passes*, what it sees each timestep is a self-consistent obs tensor. It never knows the re-permutation happened.

**(B) Dynamic sort + add mapping channels.** Store "slot N's identity at t-k" as broadcast scalars. 7 slots × N history timesteps = ~70 additional broadcast channels at N=10. Substantial. The model has to *learn* to use the mapping to cross-reference history with current state — much harder learning problem than (A).

**(C) Slot-permutation augmentation at training time.** Keep canonical-by-ID at runtime (so within-game consistency is preserved). In the dataloader, pick a random permutation of the 7 opponent slots for each training example, and apply it uniformly across all per-opponent channels in that example (broadcast scalars, spatial masks, history channels — all permuted with the same shuffle). Within a single training example, the permutation is fixed across all channels and timesteps, so internal consistency is preserved. Across examples, the permutation is random, so the model never sees a stable "slot 0 = player X" pattern. It learns to extract opponent characteristics from features (army, land, ownership shape, recent activity) rather than slot index.

#### Cost comparison

| Approach | Parser complexity | Extra channels | Model burden |
|---|---|---|---|
| Canonical-by-ID alone | Trivial | 0 | Slot index is signal-free noise; model has to learn to ignore it |
| Dynamic sort + (A) | High (state machine) | 0 | None re: ordering |
| Dynamic sort + (B) | Medium | ~70 (at N=10) | Learn to use mapping channels |
| Canonical-by-ID + (C) | Trivial + dataloader shuffle | 0 | Mild (learn to extract from features) |

Option (C) dominates the others on cost-to-benefit:
- Cheaper to build than (A) — it's a shuffle in the dataloader, not a parser state machine
- Doesn't pay (B)'s ~70-channel tax
- Captures the across-game permutation invariance that 5.02-3 §5 originally wanted, which canonical-by-ID alone gives up
- Compounds with board-symmetry augmentation (rotations + reflections), which gives up to 8× on square boards from D₄ symmetry

#### Inductive bias trade

A separate consideration: dynamic sort encodes "rank by army" as a free architectural prior — slot 0 always means "biggest army opponent," so the model can specialize early layers to "what to do about the biggest threat." Canonical-by-ID requires the model to learn the rank comparison itself.

Two arguments weighed against the dynamic-sort inductive bias even with mitigation (A) available:

1. **The "free prior" only helps if rank-by-army is the right rank to bake in.** Strategic reality is more nuanced — sometimes you care most about biggest army, sometimes closest opponent, sometimes most-aggressive-recently. Whatever single sort key you pick is privileged at the architecture level; the others have to be re-derived from features anyway.
2. **Augmentation gets you most of the benefit.** With slot-permutation augmentation, the model learns to extract opponent characteristics from features and treat slots as exchangeable. It's effectively learning "rank by whatever matters" as part of training.

#### Open assumption

The user observed that if player IDs in generals.io are effectively random across the dataset, canonical-by-ID is implicitly already getting the across-game variation that augmentation would provide. In that case, augmentation is belt-and-suspenders rather than strictly required. The original plan was to spot-check this in replay data before next session; that check was subsequently folded into future dataset prep / parser work, since the augmentation decision is locked in either way.

Either way, augmentation is so cheap that it's worth including as a default — it costs nothing to lock the door even if the neighborhood is safe.

#### What this changes downstream

- **Decision is locked.** Future obs-tensor changes can assume slot ordering is settled.
- **Augmentation work is added to the BC plan.** Should be implemented alongside board-symmetry augmentation (up to 8× from D₄ on square boards, 4× from D₂ on rectangular). Slot permutation has a 7! = 5040 theoretical maximum, but practical effective multiplier is much smaller — most permutations produce highly correlated examples. Combined practical multiplier hard to predict, but reliably exceeds either alone.
- **5.03-3 §3.2 is now historically-accurate-but-superseded.** Future readers should know that the canonical-by-ID choice was kept but augmented. This session-notes doc is the durable record.

### 3.4 Evaluation methodology

The user clarified that they themselves can play and evaluate the bot — top 25–50 FFA player on the live ladder. This is a major asset that most ML projects don't have, and shifts what evaluation can look like.

Three structures were discussed, in order of cost:

- **Held-out next-action accuracy** (continuous training-time metric). Top-1 + top-3 + per-game-phase breakdowns. Cheapest, no infrastructure, but a weak proxy for "actually plays well." Useful for "is training progressing?" and not much more.
- **Move-review on saved positions** (high information per hour of user time). Pick interesting positions from held-out games (early-game expansion, mid-game contested, endgame), have the model output its top-3 moves on each, user sanity-checks whether they make sense. Catches things like "never builds towers" or "always attacks the wrong neighbor" that no metric will surface. **No engine needed — just board states + policy outputs.** This is the highest-leverage evaluation channel for the project given the user's expertise.
- **Live head-to-head** (gold standard, most expensive). Needs deployment infrastructure. The user proposed a useful cheaper variant: **3-bot 4-player FFA** — user vs three copies of the bot, the smallest "real" FFA configuration. Caveat: identical bots create collusion artifacts (they'll mirror each other on similar states). Cheap mitigations: stochastic sampling instead of argmax, slight obs perturbation per copy (DeepNash-style), or a tiny conditioning embedding. Worth flagging that this requires the FFA-aware engine, so it's downstream of either Phase 2 or a side-quest.

The walkthrough of standard agent-evaluation methodologies in detail (perplexity, distributional metrics, simulated ELO, etc.) was pinned for a later session — best timing is once the training pipeline is producing artifacts to evaluate, so the walkthrough can be grounded in actual outputs.

### 3.5 BC plan augmentations (open suggestions to consider)

The user is open to additions to the BC plan beyond what's already in the docs. Discussed in rough order of bang-per-buck:

- **Board-symmetry augmentation**. Up to 8× on square boards from D₄ symmetry (4 rotations × 2 reflections); 4× on rectangular boards from D₂ (since 90° rotations flip dimensions). Composes with slot-permutation augmentation. Should be implemented from the start.
- **Auxiliary heads**. Predict final placement, predict who-eliminates-whom, predict army-count-in-N-turns. Multi-task regularizers. Strakam used none of these and reached SOTA at 1v1, but FFA's added strategic complexity strengthens the case.
- **Player/skill conditioning**. Learned embedding per training-player (or per skill bucket), fed as input. Trade-off: trains on a larger pool including marginal-skill players, then conditions on "strong" at inference. Adds architectural complexity but lets you trade dataset size against filter restrictiveness.
- **Endgame upsampling**. Turn-based weighting in the dataloader. Already in 5.02-3 §10's open-questions list; cheap to implement.

A bigger structural lever was flagged but explicitly deferred: **offline-RL flavors over pure BC** (Conservative Q-Learning, Implicit Q-Learning, etc.). Pure BC regresses to the expert action distribution; offline RL uses a value head to pick *better-than-average* actions from the same data. Considerably more complex; worth knowing about, only worth doing if vanilla BC plateaus disappointingly.

### 3.6 Dataset & filtering

Several pieces of new context from the user:

- **Current dataset**: ~80k 8-player FFA games with at least one strong player. ~40% have 2+ strong players.
- **Scaling possible**: easily ~200k via more scraping (1–2 weeks of effort), possibly 400–800k via deeper archive work. User is active in the generals.io Discord and knows the lead dev; community channels are an option.
- **Map size**: 90% of FFA replays are 600–900 tiles, confirming the 25×25 ballpark for architecture sizing.

Substantive points from the discussion:

- **Train only on perspectives meeting skill criterion**. Game-level filter ("at least one strong player in lobby") gates the *games*; trajectory-level filter ("this player meets skill bar") gates *whose perspective* you train on within those games. Both are needed and not redundant.
- **Opposition skill matters too**. A top player in a lobby of beginners walks over them and never demonstrates interesting late-game decisions. Filters on the *opposition* skill distribution (e.g., "all 8 players above threshold") give the most strategically rich subset, at the cost of dataset size.
- **Already past "enough samples" line.** ~80k raw → ~100–140M training samples after filtering. Standard supervised-learning scaling laws at this point: doubling the dataset yields ~5–15% validation improvement, not transformative. The leverage from more raw data is enabling stricter quality filtering, not raw sample volume. Sweet spot: ~200–400k raw replays. Above that, training time binds before learnability does.
- **Numbers worth tracking beyond raw count**: distinct strong-player count, opposition skill distribution, win/placement rate of strong players, game-length distribution, map-size distribution.
- **Filter design > more scraping** at hobby-project scale. The user's edge over Strakam's filter set isn't access to more replays — it's the ability to encode FFA-expert judgment into automatic filter rules (e.g., "drop games where the strong player wins by turn 200" probably catches weak lobbies). One worthwhile artifact for a future session: a doc capturing "what makes a generals replay genuinely useful for learning" criteria, translated into filter rules.

This material is partially covered in 5.02-3 §4 (the original filtering plan) but the trajectory-perspective question and the opposition-skill nuance are new from this session.
